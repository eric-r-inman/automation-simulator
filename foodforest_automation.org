*Section 1 --- System Overview*

This document covers everything needed to go from an empty yard to a
fully automated, remotely accessible food forest irrigation and
management system. Sections are ordered sequentially --- hardware
purchase, physical install, network setup, software environment, and
finally the application itself.

*How the System Works*

The system manages 6 irrigation zones across front and back yards. Trees
on the property are not irrigated --- they rely on Portland's abundant
winter rainfall and deep soil reserves. The system focuses water where
it is actually needed: raised veggie beds (2 zones) and shrubs with
edible and ornamental perennials (4 zones).

| *Layer*             | *Component*                                     | *Role*                                                                                              |
|---------------------+-------------------------------------------------+-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| 1 --- Physical      | Drip irrigation manifolds + tubing              | Delivers water to each of the 6 zones                                                               |
| 2 --- Controller    | OpenSprinkler OS-Pi                             | Controls which valve opens, when, and for how long                                                  |
| 3 --- Sensing       | Ecowitt GW2000 + 6 soil sensors + weather array | Measures soil moisture and local weather                                                            |
| 4 --- Hub           | Raspberry Pi 5                                  | Runs OpenSprinkler, receives Ecowitt data, runs your Rust app server, runs Tailscale                |
| 5 --- Control Panel | Elm app (compiled to JS) in browser             | Your interface, served as static assets by the Axum server --- viewable from anywhere via Tailscale |

*Network Architecture*

All devices live on the home WiFi network. The Raspberry Pi is the hub
everything connects to. Tailscale creates an encrypted tunnel so you and
the homeowner can access the control panel from anywhere without opening
firewall ports.

*Local network:* OpenSprinkler → Pi (USB/network) Ecowitt GW2000 → Pi
(HTTP push) Pi runs Rust server on port 3000

*Remote access:* Your laptop → Tailscale VPN → Pi → Rust server →
control panel in browser

*Portland Climate Context*

Portland's Mediterranean climate means almost zero irrigation is needed
October through May. The system earns its keep June through September
--- a near-drought period where raised veggie beds require daily
attention and perennial shrubs need regular supplemental water during
establishment. Trees on the property are excluded from irrigation:
Portland's winter rainfall is sufficient to maintain deep soil moisture
through the dry season for established trees, and overwatering tree
roots causes more harm than the summer dry period.

*Section 3 --- Physical Installation*

This section covers the physical irrigation installation. No electrical
work is required --- the solenoid valves run on 24VAC from the
OpenSprinkler, which itself runs on a low-voltage adapter.

*3.1 --- Zone Planning*

You have 6 zones total across two spigots. Here is the recommended zone
assignment based on plant type, since trees, shrubs, and vegetables have
fundamentally different watering needs:

Note: trees throughout the property are not irrigated. Established food
forest trees in Portland's climate are self-sufficient once their root
systems develop, relying on winter rainfall and deep soil moisture.
Irrigation resources are focused entirely on the zones that need it
most.

| *Zone* | *Yard* | *Plant Type*                                     | *Emitter Type*                     | *Typical Schedule*                                |
|--------+--------+--------------------------------------------------+------------------------------------+---------------------------------------------------|
| Zone 1 | Front  | Raised veggie beds (annuals)                     | Micro-spray or 1.0 GPH inline drip | Daily in peak summer --- these dry out fastest    |
| Zone 2 | Front  | Shrubs, edible perennials, ornamental perennials | 1.0 GPH pressure-compensating drip | Moderate --- 2-3x/week in summer once established |
| Zone 3 | Front  | Shrubs, edible perennials, ornamental perennials | 1.0 GPH pressure-compensating drip | Moderate --- 2-3x/week in summer once established |
| Zone 4 | Back   | Raised veggie beds (annuals)                     | Micro-spray or 1.0 GPH inline drip | Daily in peak summer --- these dry out fastest    |
| Zone 5 | Back   | Shrubs, edible perennials, ornamental perennials | 1.0 GPH pressure-compensating drip | Moderate --- 2-3x/week in summer once established |
| Zone 6 | Back   | Shrubs, edible perennials, ornamental perennials | 1.0 GPH pressure-compensating drip | Moderate --- 2-3x/week in summer once established |

#+begin_quote
/✔ Perennials need more water in their first season than in subsequent
years. Plan to dial back Zone 2, 3, 5, and 6 schedules after year one as
root systems establish./
#+end_quote

*3.2 --- Spigot Assembly (per spigot, repeat twice)*

At each spigot, you will build a short stack of components before the
water reaches the manifold. Order matters --- install in this sequence,
spigot outward:

1. Wrap all threaded male fittings with 2-3 wraps of Teflon tape before
   connecting.

2. Attach the 3/4" to 1/2" hose thread adapter directly to the spigot.

3. Attach the backflow preventer to the adapter. Arrow on the backflow
   preventer must point away from the spigot (toward the yard).

4. Attach the 25 PSI pressure regulator to the backflow preventer
   output.

5. Attach the Y-filter/mesh filter after the pressure regulator.

6. Attach the 3-zone solenoid manifold to the filter output.

7. Run 1/2" poly main tubing from each of the 3 manifold outputs toward
   the corresponding zone areas. Secure to the ground or fence with
   stakes.

#+begin_quote
/⚠ Hand-tighten all hose fittings plus 1/4 turn with pliers.
Over-tightening cracks plastic fittings./
#+end_quote

*3.3 --- Running Drip Lines*

*For Shrub and Perennial Zones (Zones 2, 3, 5, 6)*

8. [@8] Use the hole punch tool to punch a hole in the 1/2" main line
   wherever a 1/4" branch line needs to split off.

9. Press a barbed 1/4" fitting into the hole. Run 1/4" drip tubing from
   this fitting to each plant.

10. At the end of each 1/4" line, press in a 1.0 GPH
    pressure-compensating emitter.

11. Position emitters 4-6" from the base of each plant, not directly
    against the stem. For shrubs, one emitter per plant is sufficient in
    year one; add a second on opposite sides in year two as plants
    expand.

#+begin_quote
/✔ Perennials in their first year need more frequent watering than the
same plants in year two. Plan to revise zone schedules annually as the
planting matures./
#+end_quote

*For Raised Veggie Bed Zones (Zones 1 and 4)*

12. [@12] Run the 1/2" main supply line to the edge of each raised bed.

13. Branch off 1/2" inline drip line (pre-installed emitters every 12")
    across the width of each bed in parallel rows, spaced 12" apart.
    This creates a grid pattern that covers the entire bed surface.

14. Cap the end of each row. Cap the main line end.

15. Optionally add a micro-spray head in the center of smaller beds if
    the inline drip grid does not provide full coverage.

*3.4 --- Connecting Solenoid Valve Wires to OpenSprinkler*

Each solenoid valve on the manifold has two wires --- one common
(typically white) and one zone wire (any other color). The OpenSprinkler
OS-Pi has screw terminals for each zone plus a common terminal.

16. [@16] Run the two wires from each solenoid valve back to the Pi
    location. For front yard, use outdoor-rated wire (Rain Bird direct
    burial wire, 18-gauge, 5-conductor for 3 zones + 2 commons).

17. Connect all common (white) wires together and connect them to the
    COM terminal on the OpenSprinkler.

18. Connect each zone wire to terminals 1-6 on the OpenSprinkler, noting
    which terminal corresponds to which zone.

#+begin_quote
/⚠ Label every wire at both ends before connecting. A label maker or
masking tape with a marker works fine./
#+end_quote

*Section 4 --- Raspberry Pi Setup*

The Pi runs the entire software stack: OpenSprinkler firmware, your Rust
server, and Tailscale. Do all of this setup before mounting the OS-Pi
HAT.

*4.1 --- Install Raspberry Pi OS*

19. [@19] Download Raspberry Pi Imager on your Mac:
    https://www.raspberrypi.com/software/

20. Insert the microSD card into your Mac using a card reader.

21. Open Raspberry Pi Imager. Choose device: Raspberry Pi 5. Choose OS:
    Raspberry Pi OS Lite (64-bit) --- no desktop needed.

22. Before writing, click the gear icon (advanced options). Set hostname
    to foodforest.local, enable SSH with password authentication, set a
    username and strong password, and configure your WiFi credentials as
    a fallback (even though you'll use ethernet).

23. Write the image to the SD card. Eject and insert into the Pi.

24. Connect the Pi to your router with an ethernet cable. Power it on
    with the USB-C supply.

25. From your Mac terminal, connect: ssh [username]@foodforest.local

*4.2 --- Assign a Static Local IP*

Log into your home router admin page (usually 192.168.1.1 or
192.168.0.1) and find the DHCP reservation section. Assign the Pi's MAC
address a permanent IP --- something like 192.168.1.100. This ensures
the Pi always gets the same address, which is important since the
Ecowitt gateway pushes data to a specific IP.

*4.3 --- Install OpenSprinkler Software*

Because you are using the OS-Pi HAT, you run the OpenSprinkler software
directly on the Pi rather than a separate device.

26. [@26] SSH into the Pi.

27. Run the OpenSprinkler install script:

#+begin_quote
curl -s https://opensprinkler.com/scripts/install.sh | sudo bash
#+end_quote

28. [@28] After install, reboot: sudo reboot

29. From your Mac browser, open http://192.168.1.100:8080. You should
    see the OpenSprinkler web UI. Default password: opendoor

30. In OpenSprinkler settings, configure: number of zones (6), station
    names (Zone 1 Front Trees, etc.), and enable Weather Control. Under
    Weather Service, choose 'local' and point it to
    http://localhost:3000/weather --- this is where your Rust server
    will serve Ecowitt data in the format OpenSprinkler expects.

#+begin_quote
/✔ The OpenSprinkler API documentation is at:
https://openthings.freshdesk.com/support/solutions/articles/5000716363.
Your Rust server will use this API to open/close zones
programmatically./
#+end_quote

*4.4 --- Install Tailscale (Remote Access)*

Tailscale creates a private encrypted network between your Mac and the
Pi. Once installed, you access the Pi and your control panel using a
Tailscale IP from anywhere in the world.

31. [@31] Create a free Tailscale account at https://tailscale.com. The
    free tier supports 3 devices, which is enough.

32. On the Pi, install Tailscale:

#+begin_quote
curl -fsSL https://tailscale.com/install.sh | sh
#+end_quote

33. [@33] Authenticate the Pi to your Tailscale account: sudo tailscale
    up

34. Install Tailscale on your Mac from https://tailscale.com/download.
    Log in with the same account.

35. In the Tailscale admin console, find the Pi's Tailscale IP (looks
    like 100.x.x.x). You will use this IP to access your control panel
    remotely.

36. Test: from your Mac browser (with Tailscale running), open
    http://100.x.x.x:3000 --- this is where your Rust server will live.

#+begin_quote
/✔ To share access with the homeowner, invite her email address in the
Tailscale admin console as a user. She installs Tailscale on her phone
or laptop and can access the control panel from anywhere./
#+end_quote

*4.5 --- Mount the OS-Pi HAT*

Once the Pi software is configured and tested over SSH, power the Pi
down and mount the OS-Pi HAT:

37. [@37] Power off the Pi: sudo shutdown -h now

38. Align the OS-Pi HAT's 40-pin GPIO header with the Pi's GPIO pins.
    Press down gently but firmly until seated.

39. Connect the 24VAC power adapter to the OS-Pi's power terminals
    (labeled VAC on the board).

40. Connect your solenoid valve wires to the zone terminals.

41. Power the Pi back on. Test each zone from the OpenSprinkler web UI
    --- you should hear the solenoid valves click and water flow.

*Section 5 --- Ecowitt Sensor Setup*

*5.1 --- GW2000 Gateway*

42. [@42] Power on the GW2000 and connect it to your WiFi using the
    Ecowitt mobile app (WS View Plus, free).

43. In the Ecowitt app or GW2000 web UI, navigate to: Weather Services →
    Customized. Enable it.

44. Set the push path to: http://192.168.1.100:3000/ecowitt (your Pi's
    static IP). Set the upload interval to 60 seconds.

45. The GW2000 will now POST sensor data as form-encoded key-value pairs
    every 60 seconds. Your Rust server receives and stores this.

*5.2 --- WH51 Soil Sensors*

46. [@46] Insert 2x AA batteries into each WH51 sensor.

47. Each sensor auto-pairs with the GW2000 when powered on within range.
    Confirm in the GW2000 app that all 6 sensors appear.

48. Insert sensors into the soil in the center of each zone,
    approximately 4 inches deep. Label each with waterproof tape noting
    the zone number.

49. In the GW2000 app, rename each sensor to match its zone (e.g.,
    Zone1-FrontTrees). This name will appear in the data your Rust
    server receives.

*5.3 --- WS90 Weather Station*

50. [@50] Mount the WS90 on a pole in the most open part of the yard ---
    away from trees, walls, and structures. Manufacturer recommends
    minimum 2 meters high.

51. The WS90 is solar/battery powered. It auto-pairs with the GW2000.
    Confirm it appears in the app.

*7.3 --- Key API Routes*

The server crate exposes both hardware-facing routes and
simulation-facing routes. The Elm frontend calls them directly over
fetch. In the first iteration every hardware route is answered by the
simulated driver; switching to real hardware is a runtime config flag,
not a code change.

| *Method* | *Route*              | *Description*                                                                           |
|----------+----------------------+-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| GET      | /api/zones           | Returns all zone statuses from OpenSprinkler                                            |
| POST     | /api/zones/{id}/run  | Triggers a zone to run for N minutes                                                    |
| POST     | /api/zones/{id}/stop | Stops a running zone                                                                    |
| GET      | /api/sensors         | Returns latest soil moisture readings for all 6 zones                                   |
| POST     | /ecowitt             | Receives sensor push data from the Ecowitt GW2000                                       |
| GET      | /weather             | Serves local weather data to OpenSprinkler in its expected format                       |
| GET      | /api/plants          | Returns plant inventory (all zones)                                                     |
| POST     | /api/plants          | Adds a plant to the inventory                                                           |
| GET      | /api/schedule        | Returns upcoming watering schedule from OpenSprinkler                                   |
| GET      | /api/history         | Returns watering run history from SQLite                                                |
| GET      | /api/sim/property    | Returns the simulated property layout (dimensions, beds, zones, plants)                 |
| POST     | /api/sim/step        | Advances simulated time by N minutes (deterministic)                                    |
| POST     | /api/sim/reset       | Resets the simulation to the initial property state                                     |
| GET      | /api/sim/state       | Returns current soil moisture, reservoir, pump, and valve state of the simulated system |

*7.4 --- Deployment to the Pi*

Because the Pi runs a different CPU architecture (ARM64) than your Mac,
you can either cross-compile the Rust binary on your Mac and copy it to
the Pi, or build directly on the Pi from a clone of the repo. `just
build` produces both frontend/public/elm.js and the workspace binaries;
`nix build .#server` produces a hermetic package.

57. [@57] Option A --- cross-compile from Mac. Add the ARM64 Linux
    target to your Rust toolchain (inside nix develop):

#+begin_quote
rustup target add aarch64-unknown-linux-gnu
#+end_quote

58. [@58] Build for the Pi:

#+begin_quote
just build # or: cargo build --release --workspace && cd frontend && elm
make src/Main.elm --output public/elm.js --optimize
#+end_quote

59. [@59] Copy the binary and the static WASM bundle to the Pi:

#+begin_quote
scp target/aarch64-unknown-linux-gnu/release/automation-simulator-server
pi@192.168.1.100:~/

scp -r frontend/public pi@192.168.1.100:~/frontend-public
#+end_quote

60. [@60] Option B (recommended) --- use the NixOS module at
    nix/modules/nixos-server.nix. It declares the systemd unit, socket
    activation, watchdog, and OIDC credential loading for you. If you
    are not running NixOS on the Pi, the systemd unit below is
    equivalent:

#+begin_quote
[Unit]

Description=automation-simulator (food forest control panel)

After=network.target

[Service]

ExecStart=/home/pi/automation-simulator-server --frontend-path
/home/pi/frontend-public --base-url http://foodforest.local:3000

WorkingDirectory=/home/pi

Restart=always

Environment=LOG_FORMAT=json

[Install]

WantedBy=multi-user.target
#+end_quote

61. [@61] Enable and start the service: sudo systemctl enable
    automation-simulator-server && sudo systemctl start
    automation-simulator-server
